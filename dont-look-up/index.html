How GEO Satellites Work

What makes them uniquely vulnerable to passive interception

Geostationary satellites orbit the equator at a fixed altitude, each identified by its longitudinal orbital slot. There are 590 active GEO satellites carrying thousands of independent network links. Each satellite hosts dozens of transponders — amplifying relays operating at different frequencies and polarizations.

The core architecture is called a "bent pipe": satellites do zero encryption or decryption. They simply receive signals from the ground and rebroadcast them. A hub sends a forward link broadcast to all remote terminals in a footprint that can span a continent — roughly 10,000 km in diameter. That same broadcast falls on anyone with a dish in that footprint.

Encryption can be deployed at three layers — but often isn't:

🔒 Application layer — TLS/HTTPS ~100% for consumer web browsing
⚠️ Network layer — IPSec Only 6% of IP-carrying transponders
❌ Link layer — satellite terminal encryption Only 10% of non-TV transponders; 20% of GSE links
⚠

A 2022 NSA advisory explicitly warned: "Most of these links are unencrypted, relying on frequency separation or predictable frequency hopping rather than encryption to separate communications." This research confirms that at scale.

Unlike low-Earth orbit (Starlink, etc.), GEO satellites have no gaps in coverage — making them the backbone of critical legacy infrastructure for utilities, telecoms, military, and aviation. Many of these systems are decades old. Satellites are designed to operate for up to 15 years.

2

Align the Dish

A 1° elevation error costs 5+ dB SNR. Compass-based azimuth alignment is "effectively impossible."

This was the hardest part. A single-axis rotor sweeps along an elliptical arc — if the azimuth origin is wrong, satellites at the edges of the arc fail to lock even when central ones receive fine. The team discovered the fix hidden in the satellite feeds themselves.

1

Elevation (vertical) alignment

Any elevation error shifts the entire arc uniformly. A misalignment of just over 1° reduced SNR by more than 5 dB despite using a large 110 cm dish. Use an AR satellite-finder app to set your rough elevation, then fine-tune by watching SNR on known TV satellites.

2

Azimuth (horizontal) alignment — use the satellites themselves

Magnetic compasses and mobile apps are unreliable in urban environments. Instead, use ground-truth references hidden in the satellite feeds: some transponders broadcast their own satellite name, orbital slot (e.g., 99° W), and beam ID in DVB-SI Network Information Tables (NIT) and Service Description Tables (SDT). Cross-reference with public FTA databases like LyngSat.

The team identified 12 anchor transponders across 8 longitudes spanning 65.0° W to 129.0° W. After correcting azimuth, they gained 10° of additional arc visibility, adding 14 new satellites and 110 new transponders to the dataset.

3

Blind scan the full Ku band

Use EBSPro (Easy BlindScan Pro) — free software — to sweep 10,700–12,750 MHz across H and V polarizations. Symbol rate range: 100–70,000 KS/s. Step size: 1 MHz frequency, 1 KS/s symbol rate. Record frequency, symbol rate, polarization, FEC, and roll-off factor for every locked transponder into a structured database.

4

Raw capture: disable the MPEG-TS filter

This is the critical unlock. Linux DVB-S drivers discard any byte that isn't preceded by the MPEG-TS sync byte 0x47. This silently drops all internet data. The fix: modify and recompile the TBS-5927 driver to forward raw demodulated data directly to the demux0 device, bypassing this filter. Alternatively, in EBSPro, enable "Raw data handling (do not use Internal EBSPro's filter)" — available from version 18.0.0.2 RC onwards. The modified driver is open source on GitHub.

Use dvbv5-zap to automate locking onto each transponder from the database and recording 3–10 minutes of raw data to a .ts file.

✅

After alignment: 98.6% of all 411 transponders had SNR exceeding 6 dB. Median discarded-byte rate when parsing DVB-S2 frames: 0.1%. This is the signal quality needed for reliable protocol parsing.

Parse the Byte Stream

7 protocol stacks. 5 of them previously undocumented. "A monument to suffering."

Getting from raw bytes to Wireshark-readable pcap is where the real engineering happened. The team black-box reverse engineered the protocol universe across 346 non-TV captures (411 total minus 65 TV channels excluded).

The prior state-of-the-art tool, GSExtract (Pavur et al., 2019), was designed for maritime traffic and handled only one protocol variant. Here's how the two parsers compare on the same dataset:

🧩

The non-standard GSE quirk: 172 of 290 DVB-S2 captures use a modified GSE where: (1) the 12-bit length field is off-by-2; (2) fragment IDs use a novel 6/2 scheme instead of standard 8-bit; (3) the CRC polynomial uses initial value 0x00000000 instead of the standard 0xffffffff. GSExtract hardcodes one MATYPE value and gets this wrong for 36% of all DVB-S2 transponders it even attempts.

A

DVB-S2(X) framing: find BB headers with CRC-8

Raw captures may start mid-frame. Use the 1-byte CRC-8 over the first 9 bytes of each Base-Band header as a filter. Assume UPL/DFL lengths are multiples of 8 bits and 0 < DFL ≤ 58,112 bits. Validate candidate headers by checking that the next header also passes — if it does, you've found a true boundary. Median discarded-byte rate in valid captures: 0.1%.

B

Handle the CRC-32 variant (61% of DVB-S2 captures)

177 of 296 captures include an additional CRC-32 over the header + data field. Reverse-engineered parameters: polynomial 0x104c11db7, initial value 0x00000000. Same polynomial as GSE CRC-32 but different init value. Validate this to confirm correct frame boundaries.

C

Handle byte-swapped captures (6% of DVB-S2)

18 captures swap byte order within every 16-bit word in the data field. After un-swapping, normal IP headers become visible. This was the proprietary encoding used by AT&T Mexico, among others.

D

Blind IP header extraction for unknown protocols

For unknown encapsulation layers, scan the raw byte stream for valid IPv4 headers (correct 4-bit version, reasonable length, valid 16-bit checksum) or IPv6 (correct version + common prefix). False positive rate on random data: < 0.001%. Top decile of unknown captures: 57.2% of bytes successfully identified as belonging to IP packets.

E

Export as PCAP, open in Wireshark

When traffic is unencrypted above IP, export complete IP packets as PCAP files. Transport and application-layer protocols (HTTP, SIP, DNS, RTP, SNMP, FTP, Telnet, etc.) appear in plaintext. Use TSDuck and TShark for MPEG-TS streams, Binwalk for entropy analysis of ambiguous captures.

"This is a monument to my suffering. There are multiple variants of known protocols where they do something a little bit off the implementation. I'm told they do it on purpose." — Keegan Ryan
Example pipeline (open source on GitHub)
# 1. Capture raw .ts file from transponder
dvbv5-zap -a 0 -f <freq> -S <symbol_rate> -m DVB-S2 -o raw_capture.ts

# 2. Run multi-protocol parser
python parser.py --input raw_capture.ts --output output.pcap

# 3. Analyze in Wireshark
wireshark output.pcap

# Alternatively, run strings for quick recon
strings raw_capture.ts | grep -i "password\|config\|admin"

Bonus: Recovering a Full RSA Private Key

A device on the in-flight Wi-Fi network was leaking its own memory to the network

The in-flight Wi-Fi transponders contained something unexpected: packets with partial PEM-encoded RSA private keys. Prior work (Pavur 2022) had noticed this and assumed the missing bytes were lost to signal noise. With better parsing, the team confirmed the signal was fine — the device had a memory leak bug.

What was observed in the satellite traffic

Final 472–474 bytes of two PEM-encoded 2048-bit RSA private keys. After Base64 + DER decoding, this gave:

KNOWN: 512 least-significant bits of d_p = d mod (p-1)
KNOWN: all 1024 bits of d_q = d mod (q-1)
KNOWN: all 1024 bits of q_inv = q⁻¹ mod p
UNKNOWN: public modulus n = p·q
UNKNOWN: most-significant bits of d_p

Novel cryptanalytic recovery (new to this paper)

No prior work recovered RSA private keys from only (LSB(d_p), d_q, q_inv). The team developed a new three-step attack:

Step 1: Recover q. Since e·d_q ≡ 1 (mod q-1), there are only 2^16 possible values for the cofactor k_q. Try each until q = (e·d_q - 1)/k_q + 1 is integer and prime.

Step 2: Reduce q·q_inv - 1 (a multiple of p) from 2048 bits to ~1900 bits using elliptic curve factorization to remove small prime factors.

Step 3: Apply Coppersmith's method (dimension-30 lattice) across all 2^16 values of k_p to recover the unknown MSBs of d_p and thus recover p. Full private key recovered.

Result: Both private keys fully compromised. The keys should be considered entirely broken.

Why Is So Much Data Unencrypted?

Structural disincentives, not just negligence
1

Efficiency: encryption costs bandwidth they're paying for

Encrypted data can't be compressed. IPSec ESP + IP headers add 20–30 bytes of overhead per packet — non-trivial for VoIP. Panasonic told the researchers that enabling encryption could incur a 20–30% capacity loss. Off-grid terminals running on solar power face real power consumption constraints.

2

Economics: encryption is sold as a paid add-on

Historical pricing wars led satellite providers to unbundle encryption as a separate license fee for the terminal's crypto subsystem. There is no economic incentive for providers to protect customer privacy, and a direct disincentive for customers to pay extra. By contrast, satellite TV scrambling has been standard for 40 years — because it protects revenue.

3

Visibility: nobody could audit their own satellite links

No auditing tools exist that allow vendors or customers to check whether their satellite backhaul is actually encrypted. Multiple organizations the team contacted believed they were encrypting. In several cases, misconfiguration or software updates had silently disabled encryption — the network continued working with no error indication. The researchers had to validate that fixes worked, because the organizations had no way to verify themselves.

4

Abstraction: layers of commercial contracts obscure accountability

Satellite network connectivity often passes through multiple service provider layers. The A 2021 US Executive Order and 2022 follow-up memo mandate zero-trust architecture for US government infrastructure — requiring encryption on all internal networks. Even with this mandate, the military had traffic running in the clear through a commercial satellite provider chain.

5

Internal networks treated as trusted by default

Organizations treat satellite backhaul as just another transparent link in their private network. Since internal networks aren't encrypted end-to-end (defense in depth is hard to deploy), adding a satellite hop simply exposes whatever was already in plaintext — now broadcast to a continent-sized footprint.

"It never occurred to me to think that a broadcaster would accidentally broadcast clear text call contents to an entire continent. Perhaps we can update our threat model." — Nadia Heninger
⛔

Authentication risk: Unencrypted SCADA and DNP3 industrial control traffic also lacks authentication. An attacker who could broadcast to the satellite uplink — e.g., with a drone positioned between a terminal and the satellite — could potentially inject commands into the internal networks of utilities and critical infrastructure. CFE and similar organizations were exposed to this risk.
